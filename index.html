The Championship
Green Wins Its First Title Since 2017
The Green Machine is back on top. Michigan's 69-63 win over UConn gives Lia 133 FDM points and the 2026 championship — Green's first title since 2017 and third overall, pulling Lia into sole possession of second place on the all-time wins list behind Luke. It wasn't pretty: Michigan held UConn to 30.8% shooting and made 25 of 28 free throws in a grinder that looked nothing like the Wolverines' five previous blowouts. Yaxel Lendeborg, playing through a sprained MCL and rolled ankle, went just 4-for-13 from the field but still scored 13. Elliot Cadeau — a transfer from North Carolina who was "really down on myself" a year ago — scored 19 points and was named the Final Four's Most Outstanding Player. UConn cut it to four with under eight minutes left, but Michigan answered every run. Trey McKenney's dagger three with 1:50 remaining sealed it. Michigan ends its own 37-year championship drought and the Big Ten's 26-year conference-wide drought. Dan Hurley's bid for three titles in four years falls just short.

We don't care about finishing 2nd or 3rd in points if you were mathematically eliminated before the final game. Power rankings are the order of who still had a chance to win, and Joe may have finished 4th overall in points, but in reality was the last person standing between Lia and the title. That's 2nd. Joe was swinging for the fences, and we respect that — even though 2nd is just as good as last. If you don't like it, you can start your own website to judge everyone.

FDM vs. FDM
The Head-to-Head Wars
In a league where eight players draft from the same 64-team field, direct collisions are inevitable. This year, they were everywhere — and they decided the title.

Chad vs. Everyone (6-5 against 6 different opponents). No player was more entangled in head-to-head action. Chad's teams played 11 games against other FDM-rostered opponents — more direct confrontations than any other player — in every round from the first through the Final Four. Chad beat Joe twice (Alabama over Texas Tech, Arizona over Arkansas), split with Luke and Box, and beat Bob1 in the Elite Eight when Arizona knocked off Purdue. But the two losses that mattered most were both against the same team: Lia's Michigan beat Chad's Alabama in the Sweet 16, then beat Chad's Arizona in the Final Four. The team that eliminated Chad from FDM was the same team that won it all for Lia.

Joe's UConn vs. Luke's Entire Roster (4-0). The most lopsided head-to-head in recent FDM memory, and maybe ever. UConn eliminated Furman (R1, within 5 late), UCLA (R2), Michigan State (S16, came back from 19 down to within 1), and Duke (E8, Mullins buzzer-beater). Four of Luke's eight teams, killed one per round, by the same school. Three of the four games were decided in the final minutes. If any single one goes the other way, the entire FDM landscape is different.

Box's Iowa vs. Lia's Florida (24 FDM points). The single biggest game swing of the tournament. A 9-seed knocking off the defending champion 1-seed, worth 24 points, vaulting Box from 7th to 2nd in one afternoon and handing Lia a devastating loss. The Iowa-Nebraska Sweet 16 matchup (Box vs. Chad) was also a beauty — a Big Ten rubber match between regular-season rivals, with former Hawkeye Pryce Sandfort now wearing Nebraska's jersey.

Bob1 vs. Lia (2-1). Bob1 actually won the head-to-head — St. John's beat Northern Iowa, Purdue beat Texas — but Lia's Michigan beat Bob1's Saint Louis and, more importantly, outlasted everyone else. Bob1 won the battles but lost the war.
2026 vs. History
Where This Year Fits in 16 Editions
Total points: 729 — second-lowest ever. Only 2025 (711) was lower. The two lowest-scoring FDM years in history are now back-to-back, and that probably isn't a coincidence. FDM points are generated by upsets — when low seeds beat high seeds, the gap between them inflates the score. So when scoring drops league-wide, it means the chalk is holding. The question is why. Is the selection committee getting better at seeding? Is NIL money consolidating talent at the top programs, making it harder for mid-majors to pull Cinderella runs? Is the transfer portal creating superteams that simply don't lose to 12-seeds anymore? Two years of data isn't a trend, but it's enough to raise the question. If 2027 comes in under 750 again, FDM may need to reckon with the possibility that the game has changed.

Winning score: 133 — third-lowest ever. Only Lucas's 124 in 2025 and Bob1's 127 in 2015 produced lower champions. For context, Bob2 won 2010 with 292 points and Luke won 2022 with 239. Lia's 133 would have finished outside the top 3 in most prior years.

Standard deviation: 33.5 — fourth-most competitive year. Only 2025 (23.2), 2019 (30.8), and 2015 (33.4) had tighter fields. The spread from 2nd to 5th place was just 16 points — Chad at 118, Bob1 at 104, Joe at 103, Box at 102. That's absurdly compact for a game decided by upsets and chaos.

Lucas's 16 is tied for the second-lowest individual score ever. Box scored 3 in 2014 — the all-time low that may never be broken — and Lia scored 16 in 2024. Lucas ties that mark. In a fitting bit of symmetry, Lia went from scoring 16 two years ago to winning with 133 this year. Four of Lucas's eight teams were eliminated in the first round, and the two headliners — Houston (2) and Gonzaga (3) — scored just 11 and 5 FDM points before being eliminated in the Sweet 16 and second round, respectively.

First time the championship game determined the FDM winner. In 16 editions, the FDM title had always been clinched — or mathematically sealed — before the final game of the NCAA Tournament. Not this year. Michigan vs. UConn was Lia vs. Joe, and neither knew the outcome until the final buzzer. That's never happened before, and it may never happen again.
